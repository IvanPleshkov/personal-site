Transformer embeddings have a well-known property — **anisotropy**. They
concentrate inside a narrow cone on the unit sphere, a few directions
carry most of the variance, and the rest carry almost nothing. What's
less discussed: part of this anisotropy is **non-linear**, and a linear
PCA can't see it.

This post is about an autoencoder that can. Encoder is linear (top-d
PCA), decoder is quadratic (polynomial lift + Ridge OLS). No SGD, no
epochs. Encoder is plain PCA, decoder is one `np.linalg.solve`. On 100K
vectors at d=100 the entire pipeline runs in a couple of minutes on
CPU.

```
encoder:   p = (V - V̄) @ Q                   # linear, via PCA
decoder:   V̂ = polynomial_lift(p) @ W + b    # quadratic, via Ridge
residual:  V_resid = V - V̂
```

I ran this on eight embeddings — from classical word vectors all the
way to OpenAI ada-002, CLIP, and sentence-T5:

## 1. The idea — PCA is linear, the data isn't

PCA is, in essence, a closed-form linear autoencoder: the encoder
projects a vector onto the top-d eigenvectors of the covariance matrix,
the decoder applies the inverse projection plus the mean. Both encode
and decode are matrix multiplications. Linear operations — and that's
enough, **as long as the data itself sits inside a linear ellipsoid**.

Modern neural-network embeddings don't sit in a linear ellipsoid. They
lie on a non-linear manifold — the well-known **cone effect** in
transformers: the cloud is concentrated in a narrow cone on the sphere
and is heavily non-linearly structured inside that cone.

</picture>

A linear decoder can't track that curvature — it only catches
projections along the orthogonal eigenvectors of the covariance.
Whatever sits along non-linear directions is invisible to it.

The idea of this post: **switch to a non-linear decoder**, but in a way
that keeps the pipeline closed-form (no neural network training, no
hyperparameter search). Concretely: lift the PCA projection into all
polynomial monomials of degree 2 and run linear regression in this
expanded space. That's equivalent to a quadratic decoder. **Moving up
into a higher-dimensional linear space** compensates for the
non-linearity of the data, and the whole pipeline collapses to a single
`np.linalg.solve` on top of an ordinary PCA.

## 2. Disclaimer — old method, new mode

After I'd already finished all the experiments, it turned out that
regression on polynomial PCA features is the long-known **PPCR**
(Polynomial Principal Component Regression), described back in
[1989](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0169743989801169)
in chemometrics. PPCR is applied as a **supervised** regression of PCA
features onto an external target `Y` (reactor yield, toxicity, profit).
It hasn't really made it into the modern ML toolbox.

What I'm contributing:

1. **Substituting `Y := X`** — the input vector itself becomes the
   target, and that substitution turns a supervised regression into a
   self-reconstruction, i.e. an autoencoder. I haven't found this
   closed-form autoencoder applied to anisotropy in transformer
   embeddings anywhere.
2. **Applying it to modern neural-network embeddings** (transformers,
   CLIP, deep image features) and measuring R² along with the
   correlation between the gain and anisotropy.

So the method itself is decades old, just from a non-ML field. Turning
it into an autoencoder via the `Y := X` substitution and validating
that on modern embeddings is what's new here. Treat this post as an
experimental measurement of a known method in a new mode (autoencoding).

## 3. Linear PCA — baseline and its ceiling

The standard way to measure anisotropy is the condition number of the
**centered** covariance, `λ_max / λ_min`. One nuance: the tail of the
full spectrum sits at the level of floating-point noise (~10⁻¹⁵), and
including it makes cond explode. So everywhere in this post cond is
taken over the top-200 directions — a stable quantity that reflects
real structure rather than FP noise. On DBpedia-OpenAI (D=1536, N=100K,
L2-normalized to the unit sphere) `cond ≈ 72` — a heavily elongated
ellipsoid.

For the baseline I take top-d PCA — a closed-form linear autoencoder:
the encoder projects a vector onto the top-d eigenvectors of the
covariance, the decoder applies the inverse projection plus the mean.
`V_proj ∈ ℝ^d` is stored per-vector (`d × 2` bytes in fp16),
`Q ∈ ℝ^{D × d}` is a global matrix, amortized across N. The explained
variance ratio `R²` on DBpedia at different `d`:

| d   | R²    | cond(V_resid) | bytes/vector (V_proj fp16) |
|-----|-------|---------------|----------------------------|
| 50  | 0.44  | 5.94          | 100                        |
| 100 | 0.61  | 4.21          | 200                        |
| 200 | 0.77  | 2.73          | 400                        |

At `d=100`, 39% of the variance ends up in an unstructured residual.
PCA is a linear operator, and that remainder is something it
geometrically can't capture.

## 4. Architecture — linear encoder, quadratic decoder

Back to the idea: "non-linear decoder by moving into a
higher-dimensional linear space." This is a standard technique in
regression theory. If you want to fit data with a quadratic function,
you can either fit the quadratic model directly (non-linear
optimization), or — much more conveniently — **first compute all the
quadratic combinations of the inputs and then run an ordinary linear
regression on them**. That's exactly what a **polynomial lift** does:
a low-dimensional coordinate is lifted into a higher-dimensional space
where the quadratic dependency turns into a linear one.

A concrete example. Take a two-dimensional vector `p` with coordinates
`p₁` and `p₂` — just a pair of numbers. The degree-2 lift turns it
into a 6-dimensional vector:

```
lift([p₁, p₂]) = [1,   p₁, p₂,   p₁², p₁·p₂, p₂²]
                  ↑    ↑   ↑     ↑    ↑      ↑
                bias  linear     quadratic
                                 (including cross-terms)
```

Any linear combination of these six components is a **quadratic
function** of the original `p₁, p₂`. In other words, **linear
regression on the lift = quadratic regression in the original space**.
No non-linear optimization needed — it all reduces to a closed-form
Ridge OLS.

Now we apply this to our case. Encoder — top-d PCA
(`p = (V − V̄) @ Q`, `p ∈ ℝ^d`). We lift `p` into all monomials up to
degree 2 (bias, linear, pairwise products, and squares):

```python
def polynomial_lift(p, degree=2):
    features = [1.0]                              # bias
    features.extend(p)                            # degree 1
    for i in range(len(p)):
        for j in range(i, len(p)):
            features.append(p[i] * p[j])          # degree 2
    return np.array(features)
```

Lift dimension is `M = (d+1)(d+2)/2`. For `d=100` that's `M = 5151`,
for `d=200` it's `M = 20301`. The decoder is a linear regression from
the M-dimensional lift back to the D-dimensional original vector.
Despite being "ordinary linear regression", the resulting map
`p → V̂` is already quadratic — exactly what we wanted.

Training the decoder is one `np.linalg.solve`:

```python
def fit_decoder(P, V, lam=1e-3):
    L = polynomial_lift(P, degree=2)               # (N, M)
    G = L.T @ L + lam * np.trace(L.T @ L) / L.shape[1] * np.eye(L.shape[1])
    W = np.linalg.solve(G, L.T @ V)
    return W
```

What we end up with is a **polynomial autoencoder**:

  <img src="/blog/polynomial-autoencoder/images/ae_schema_en_light.svg" alt="Polynomial autoencoder schema: V → PCA → p → poly lift → L → Ridge OLS → V̂; V − V̂ → V_resid" />
</picture>

- **encoder** — closed-form linear (PCA), no training,
- **decoder** — closed-form quadratic (Ridge OLS on the polynomial
  lift), trained by a single `np.linalg.solve` on ~5K-20K features,
- **reconstruction** — `V̂ = polynomial_lift(p) @ W + b`,
- **residual** — `V_resid = V − V̂` (a natural side-product).

No backprop, no learning rate, no batch size, no epochs. No non-linear
activations. On N=100K with d=100 — minutes on CPU.

### Why this combination is rare in modern ML

Curious thing: **polynomial lifts** are uncommon in modern ML. They
historically developed in two distinct disciplines that barely overlap.

On one side — **kernel methods**: SVM, Gaussian processes, kernel PCA.
There the lift is implicit, hidden behind the kernel trick — we never
actually compute the `M = (d+1)(d+2)/2` monomials, we evaluate the
kernel function `(1 + x·y)²` directly. It works on small datasets but
doesn't scale to millions of points, and it has therefore fallen out
of DL-embedding practice.

On the other side — **classical applied statistics**: response surface
methodology ([Box & Wilson
1951](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Response_surface_methodology)),
PPCR ([Vong, Geladi, Wold & Esbensen
1989](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0169743989801169))
from chemometrics. There the lift is explicit, the regression is
linear, everything works — but it's only used as a **supervised**
regression onto an external target. It barely registers in modern ML
practice either.

In the autoencoder literature, by contrast, decoders are built as
**trainable neural networks**. I haven't run into closed-form
polynomial decoders in either the transformer-anisotropy literature
([All-But-The-Top](https://openreview.net/forum?id=HkuGJ3kCb), BERT-flow,
ZCA whitening, [Stable Anisotropic
Regularization](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=dbQH9AOVd5) — all linear)
or in the general autoencoder literature.

So our configuration sits at the intersection of three traditions:
explicit polynomial lift + autoencoder mode + modern neural-network
embeddings.

### One technical detail

Raw `V_proj` after PCA has huge anisotropy along its own coordinates
(the first axis carries about 50× the variance of the last) — that's
bad for Ridge: the large axes dominate the solution and the small ones
are starved of weight. The fix is per-axis normalization to unit
variance:

```python
V_normed = V_proj / np.sqrt(eigvals[-d:])
P = V_normed * (0.9 / np.linalg.norm(V_normed, axis=1).max())
```

The global multiplier `0.9 / max-norm` keeps `‖p‖ ≤ 0.9` — a safety
margin for numerical stability when squaring. It doesn't affect R²,
but it's numerically cleaner.

## 5. Holdout measurement

With `M = 20K` features and `N = 100K` training vectors, the first
question is: is this overfitting? The standard sanity check — an
80/20 train/test split. All PCA statistics (centered mean, eigvecs,
std, ball scale) and Ridge weights come from train only. We apply them
to the held-out test set, and compute R² against the centered baseline
`(V_test - mean_train)²` — the same baseline for both linear PCA and
the polynomial decoder.

```python
rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
perm = rng.permutation(N)
V_train = V[perm[:80_000]]
V_test  = V[perm[80_000:]]

stats   = fit_pca_stats(V_train, d)
P_train = apply_pca_init(V_train, stats)
P_test  = apply_pca_init(V_test, stats)

L_train = polynomial_lift(P_train, degree=2)
L_test  = polynomial_lift(P_test,  degree=2)

W, b    = fit_ridge(L_train, V_train, lam=1e-3)

R²_train = 1 - sse(V_train, L_train @ W + b) / sst(V_train)
R²_test  = 1 - sse(V_test,  L_test  @ W + b) / sst(V_test)
```

Results:

| d   | M     | R²_train | R²_test | Δ overfit | cond(V_resid)_test |
|-----|-------|----------|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| 50  | 1326  | 0.581    | 0.567   | +0.014    | 4.81                |
| 100 | 5151  | 0.762    | 0.729   | +0.033    | 3.42                |
| 200 | 20301 | 0.907    | 0.829   | +0.078    | 2.24                |

Compared to a linear PCA at the same `d`:

| d   | PCA R² | **Polynomial R²_test** | gain          |
|-----|--------|------------------------|---------------|
| 50  | 0.44   | **0.567**              | **+12.7 p.p.** |
| 100 | 0.61   | **0.729**              | **+12.0 p.p.** |
| 200 | 0.77   | **0.829**              | **+5.9 p.p.**  |

The quadratic decoder beats linear PCA on holdout everywhere — by
6–13 percentage points of variance at the same per-vector budget.

**What this tells us about DBpedia:**

- **`d=100` — the sweet spot.** R²_test = 0.73 versus 0.61 for PCA —
  +12 p.p. at the same 200 bytes per vector. Overfit is minimal
  (+0.03). Equivalent to having a PCA with `d ≈ 160`.
- **`d=200` — ceiling and overfit.** Train R² = 0.91, test R² = 0.83.
  Δ = 8 p.p. M = 20K ≈ 5× training vectors — Ridge still holds, but
  the margin is shrinking. On indices with 1M+ vectors the overfit
  should collapse, but we don't have that much data.
- **Residual anisotropy doesn't overfit.** `cond(V_resid)` is a
  stable characteristic — train/test discrepancy ≤ 0.1 at every `d`.
  "The residual becomes isotropic" is a real property of the data, not
  a fitting artifact. At `d=100`, cond drops from 72 to 3.4.

One caveat about N. For the four transformers in the headline table
(sentence-T5, MiniLM, mpnet, CLIP) I encoded a 20K subsample of DBpedia
text to save time. At that N, the polynomial decoder starts overfitting
once `d ≳ 100`, and the optimal `d` shifts toward smaller values
(hence `d` of 40–50 in those rows, not 100). On the full N=100K the
numbers in those rows would be a touch higher, but the **qualitative
gap** between anisotropic and isotropic embeddings doesn't depend on N.

## 6. Compressing embeddings

The autoencoder by itself doesn't compress anything. It just
re-packages the vector into the pair `(latent coordinate p, residual
V_resid)` — the same information, just in a different form. To get
real compression, you still need to quantize the residual. And here
the anisotropy-killing property of our autoencoder pays off nicely: on DBpedia
at `d=100`, `cond(V_resid)` falls from 72 down to 3.4 — an almost
isotropic residual. Linear PCA at the same `d` gives cond=4.21 — also
more isotropic than the input, but visibly less so.

Google Research recently published
[**TurboQuant**](https://research.google/blog/turboquant-redefining-ai-efficiency-with-extreme-compression/) —
a quantizer with a fixed random rotation that on isotropic
distributions **comes close to the theoretical compression limit**
(Shannon rate-distortion for Gaussians). The autoencoder removes the
anisotropy, TurboQuant efficiently quantizes the now-isotropic
residual.

The full loop looks like this:

```
V  ──►  encoder (PCA)  ──►  p
        │
        decoder (poly+Ridge)  ──►  V̂
        │
V  −  V̂  ──►  V_resid  ──►  TurboQuant  ──►  compact code
```

If you're curious, you can try this yourself. Pull up PCA + polynomial
lift (code in the
[repo](https://github.com/IvanPleshkov/poly-autoencoder)), run a
2-bit TurboQuant on the residual, and measure recall on an ANN-search
task. You should get something reasonable: the hybrid `V̂ + 2-bit
Quant(V_resid)` at a budget of ~600 bytes/vector gives R@10 ≈ 0.96 on
100 queries from DBpedia.

But that recall will most likely fall short of a **modified TurboQuant
with anisotropy compensation** — the version that ships in
[Qdrant](https://qdrant.tech/) 1.18.0, currently being prepared for
release. I'll cover that one, along with all the other tricks I used
while implementing TurboQuant for vector search in Qdrant, in the
**next post**.

## 7. What to try next

Two directions worth digging into:

- **Fill the gap between anisotropic and isotropic models.** We have
  6 points on the anisotropic side (cond ∈ [48, 72], gain
  +9.5–13.4 p.p.) and 2 on the isotropic side (cond ≤ 7, gain
  ≤ 2 p.p.). Nothing in between. Are there models with cond ~ 10–30,
  and what does the gain look like there — a smooth transition or a
  hard threshold? To draw a real curve you'd want ~10–20 embeddings,
  with the most interesting points right in the middle of the gap.
- **Polynomial lift of degree 3.** Here the decoder works on a basis
  of `M = (d+1)(d+2)/2` quadratic monomials. Lifting up to cubic gives
  `M = O(d³)` and could in theory catch even more elaborate non-linear
  structure. The cost is the explosion of the basis (at d=100 already
  `M ≈ 175K`) and the risk of overfit on small N. The interesting
  question is: at what d/N does degree 3 actually start to help, and
  is there a maximum useful degree, and if so, does it depend on the
  embedding?
